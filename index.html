The Origins of the H system

Created in the year 1900, the Interborough Rapid Transit company, or IRT, was created to be in charge of the city's first ever subway line, which ran from City Hall on the Lower East Side to 145th Street on Manhattan's Upper West Side. The line switched from the east side of Manhattan to the west side via 42nd street, essentially serving as the template for the IND Lexington, Broadway, and 42nd Street Lines. It should be noted that elevated lines did predate this invention, as discussed in the BRT section. This was the just first underground people mover system in New York City.

Less than a year after the 1904 subway opened, the IRT opened it's connection for 110th street into Harlem and the Bronx, which is used by present day 2 and 3 trains. Additionally, the originally subway's terminus at 145th street was also extended into the Bronx at 242nd Street, which is currently used by the 1 train. Looking downwards, a seventh avenue line was opened in 1908 that connected 42nd Street to a new loop terminal at South Ferry. The original subway was also extended from City Hall to the South Ferry.

With the completion of the Lexington Avenue line, or the line that runs on the Upper East Side by the IRT along with a new set of tunnels into Brooklyn, the IRT proceeded to rearrange it's services. A "H" system of services resulted, where trains would either run on the IRT Seventh Avenue Line onto the Broadway Line into the Bronx, or trains would run on the Lexington avenue line. In both cases, the subways run on elevated tracks in the Bronx, connecting the new subway to the pre-existing Ninth, Sixth, Third, and Second Avenue elevated railroads. Equally as important, the 42nd street connection between the Seventh Avenue and Lexington lines was converted into the 42nd Street shuttle.

Diagram of the South Ferry Loop illustrating that the loop is composed of two loops. The inner loop was served by Lexington Avenue trains, but is currently used by 5 trains to turn around. The outer loop was used by Seventh Avenue trains but was abandoned in 2017 in favor of the new South Ferry station on the lower level, served by the 1 train (some 5 trains terminate at Bowling Green rush hours). When the station was in use, the inner and outer platforms operated independently (no transfer).


Image of the Old South Ferry Station's outer loop. Notice the sections of platform that seem detached from the rest of the platform by extending out to the train. These are known as gap extenders, and are used when the station exists on a curve that creates a gap in larger subway cars (to be discussed later). Gap fillers can be retracted or extended, but it is simply more faster to have non curved stations, hence the existence of new City Hall and South Ferry stations. The only station in the system to still have gap fillers is the 14th Street-Union Square station on the IRT Lexington Line platforms.
