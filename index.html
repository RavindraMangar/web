The IRT Flushing Line

Out of the entire A division (numbered lines) of NYCT, the IRT Flushing Line, represented by the purple service emblem and the number seven, is the youngest IRT line. The Interborough Rapid Transit Company created the line in 1915-1928 as a part of a significant moment in NYC transit history known as the Dual Contracts, which was an agreement made by the Interborough Rapid Transit Company, the Brooklyn Rapid Transit Company, and the city to extend and build new subway lines in Brooklyn and Queens.

However, a part of the Flushing actually pre-dates the IRT. The Steinway Tunnel under the East River was bult in 1885 by the East River Tunnel Railroad, which abandoned the plan for the tunnel after the tunnels electrification in the early 1900s. It was then when the IRT took over and extended the tunnel to Hunters Point Avenue in 1915. The following year, the line was brought onto an elevated structure at the Queensboro Plaza station, which was soon to be the prized jewel of Queens.

Under the Dual Contracts, both the IRT, and the reformed Brooklyn-Manhattan Transit Company from the ruins of the BMT, created the Flushing and Astoria Lines respectively. Queensboro Plaza became a mega-complex, with trains coming from the Steinway Tunnel, BMT 59th Street Tunnels, and the IRT's Second Avenue Elevated over the Queensborough bridge, and outwards onto the IRT's Flushing Line or the IRT's Astoria Line. The BMT ran a shuttle over their own Astoria line directly parallel to the IRT. In the plan for the complex, there was also a plan for a BMT Crosstown line to connect to their Jamaica Elevated at the entrance of the Williamsburg Bridge.

The range 1925-1927 was huge for the IRT Flushing line. In the same range, the IRT Astoria Line was converted for BMT use, ending the BMT shuttle service and starting BMT service from Astoria-Ditmars Boulevard onto points south in Brooklyn. At the same time, points east of Queenosboro Plaza into the main street of Flushing was completed. Additionally, the line was extended from Grand Central onto Fifth Avenue in 1926, and to Times Square in 1927. This was just in time seeing that the World's Fair would come to Corona Park just a little more than ten years later.

Presently, with the IRT Flushing Line running from 34th Street-Hudson Yards to Flushing-Main Street, the 7 line has earned the name of the "International Express" for serving multiple high density and diverse neighborhoods in succession, including Midtown, Sunnyside, Jackson Heights, Mets-Willets Point, and Flushing. Even with the implementation of Communications Based Train Control, or CBTC on the line along with Automatic Train Operation in 2019, allowing the line to see trains every single minute during rush hours pre-pandemic, 7 trains were still filled to capacity and are pretty much at their limit. Thus, I introduce the LaGuardia AirTrain plan.

The LaGuardia AirTrain Plan

Headed by Governor Andrew Cuomo and the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, the LGA AirTrain Plan is a proposal to connect LaGuardia airport to a designated area in Queens for greater accessibility. In a study released on how the LGA Airtrain in early January, it was determined that out of Jackson Heights and Mets-Willet's Point, the AirTrain will be built on Mets-Willet's Point.

The new AirTrain will be similar in structure to the already existing JFK Airport, which currently connects all the terminals of JFK Internation Airport and also features two branches, one to Howard Beach in connetion to the IND (Independent Subway, the third company out of three founding companies that made up the subway other than the IRT and the BMT) Rockaway Line and the IND Archer Avenue Line along with the LIRR's Jamaica Station. Each station in the LGA AirTrain's Terminal zone will have a parking lot and areas to connect to buses.

The Issue of the LGA AirTrain

As you may have figured out by the title of this webpage, I have a bone to pick with the alignment of the LGA AirTrain that the developers have chosen, and it has to with the IRT Flushing Line.

For context on the two alignments, Jackson Heights was a potential candidate because of the connections it has, for which it is a major transfer point for the IRT Flushing Line and the IND Queens Boulevard Line. Both of these lines are a part of New York City Transit, or in other words the subway system. Similarly, Mets-Willets point was also a candidate because of it's connections, it being the IRT Flushing Line, and the LIRR Port Washington Branch. One of these connections is a subway line, and the other is a commuter railroad. According to the website given above for the thinking behind the superiority of Mets-Willets Point to Jackson Heights, the travel time goes as follows.

Notice that the picture illustrates a predicted travel time of thirty minutes or less using the Long Island Rail Road. If we only consider the LIRR Port Washington Branch, we can look at the areas that it serves to see who it will benefit. In the City Terminal Zone, the branch connects to New York Pennsylvania Station, and will soon connect to Grand Central Terminal. To the east we have...Port Washington, along with Flushing as a major station. The Port Washington Branch is the only LIRR Branch that doesn't connect to Jamaica.

Well lets look at the IRT Flushing Line. You will see that the IRT Flushing Line also serves NY Penn, GCT, and Flushing! To know which service riders would use more, we can just look at the price and the composition of the branches. The subway has a base fare of $2.75, while the LIRR from Penn to Willets is about $10, making the 7 better. For the composition, the IRT already has headways of seven minutes. As for the Port Washington Branch, it is single tracked in Long Island, meaning that both directions are using one track instead of two. This severely impacts the frequently at which Port Washington trains can run, up to about every 20 to 30 minutes. Therefore, the 7 train would come more frequently. Resultantly, more people would probably use the IRT Flushing Line compared to the LIRR Port Washington Branch, creating a loophole in the current alignment.

The consequences of having the AirTrain serve the IRT Flushing Line without a suffcient service to back the flushing line up? I think this political cartoon summarizes the situation pretty accurately.

The IRT Flushing Line already serves so many high density residental neighborhoods, and the LGA AirTrain is bound to have heaps of passengers. The Flushing Line cannot handle the extra passengers at this rate. At least with the Jackson Heights alignment, the riders would have been split between the Flushing Line and the Queens Boulevard Line (though even this is not a great solution because the Queens Boulevard Line is also at capacity and is a major source of overcrowding, but CBTC coming to line will allow for more trains being run on it to somewhat solve the problem). As it already is, the IRT Flushing Line is barely able to serve everything that it serves already. So what is the solution? I and the people I have asked from the Stuyvesant Transit and Urbanism Association along with Brooklyn Tech's counterpart propose the extension of the BMT Astoria Line. Not only is the line closer to Manhattan than the IRT Flushing Line alignment, but the line is far from capacity. It will also be the best for riders in terms of fares with a direct subway connection to LaGuardia Airport. All we need to do is extend the N line by less than five blocks to reach LaGuardia.

With that, I make my final point against this AirTrain plan. The Astoria Line will be better in terms of fares and travel time. In addition, riders coming from Jamaica and the Rockaways will still have access to the Q70 bus service, and riders from Uptown and Metro-North will continue to have the M60. As such, coverage will be practically the entirely of Long Island, Queens, and Brooklyn, with quickly access to Manhattan, the Bronx, and even Staten Island. Best of all, an extension of the Astoria Line will not gridlock the system, saving the IRT Flushing Line from the impending doom waiting for it right around the corner.
